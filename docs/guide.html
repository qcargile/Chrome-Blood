Player Guide

Chrome & Blood changes how most of the game plays. This page covers what you need to know to get going — the controls, the systems that work differently, and how to deal with them.

This is the how-to-play side of things. If you run into a specific problem instead, the FAQ has direct answers, and if you want to change how anything works, almost everything is adjustable in the in-game Mod Settings menus.

00 · GETTING STARTED

Getting Started

A few things will catch you off guard if you walk in expecting vanilla. Here's what to do about the common ones.

  • Play on Very Hard. The list is balanced around it. The normal difficulty slider still works on top of everything if you want to nudge it one way or the other.
  • If you're dying a lot early, that's mostly the point. Combat is genuinely lethal here. Use cover, don't run into the open, and pick your fights. If it's more punishing than you want, you can turn Damage Scaling down in Mod Settings.
  • Money is tight at the start. You earn eddies from jobs — gigs, contracts, and bounties — not from looting, since most loot sells for almost nothing.
  • You can't quickhack enemies on sight. You have to breach a network first: knock someone out and breach them directly, or breach an access point.
  • On the Immersive profile, a lot of the HUD is hidden or locked behind cyberware. Install what it asks for, and if the minimap won't show up, toggle it with F6 or F8.
  • You can't craft just anywhere. Crafting happens at a stash until you unlock the perk for it.
  • If fixers aren't calling, do some NCPD activity in that district first. That's what gets the local fixer to start sending you work.
  • If your loot icons vanished, that's Muted Markers cleaning up the screen. Turn the ones you want back on in its settings.
  • If it's too dark to see at night, raise Shadow Boost under ENV Tuner in Mod Settings.

02 · COMBAT

Combat & Survival

Fights are quick and dangerous for everyone. Enemies flank, take cover, fall back, and call in reinforcements, and they spot you faster than they used to — so where you stand and what you stand behind matters a lot more than it does in vanilla. You can go down in a couple of hits, but so can they.

Ammo isn't unlimited anymore, and reloading is manual. A reload also throws away whatever rounds were left in the magazine, so reload when you mean to rather than out of habit.

Your armor has an integrity value — the blue percentage next to your health. It wears down over time, and you repair it at a ripperdoc on the cyberware screen.

On the Immersive profile you've also got survival needs to keep up with, and stacking too much cyberware carries a humanity cost that can push you toward cyberpsychosis.

03 · HACKING & STEALTH

Hacking & Stealth

Hacking works differently here. You can't scan an enemy and immediately quickhack them — quickhacks stay locked until you breach the network they're on. Knock someone unconscious and breach them directly, or breach a nearby access point. As your Cyberdeck and Intelligence improve, more of your quickhacks open back up, so it gets easier as you build into it.

Enemy netrunners are out there too, and they react if you lean on hacking too hard — slowing your uploads, locking networks back down, and generally making your life difficult. Ease off, or deal with them directly.

Stealth is tighter than vanilla. Enemies notice more and they react to bodies, so a corpse left out in the open can give you away. Drag them somewhere out of sight.

04 · TIME DILATION

Time Dilation & Sandevistans

The main thing to know going in: enemies can now move inside your time dilation. Hitting your Sandevistan doesn't freeze the world and make you safe the way it does in vanilla — anyone with the right cyberware keeps fighting, so you still have to dodge and use cover.

Past that, there are eleven different Sandevistan operating systems and they each play completely differently — a few don't slow time at all. Slowing time hard enough strains your body, and queuing quickhacks in scanner slow-mo isn't free anymore either. Try a few and run whichever one fits how you play; the full roster and what each one does is on the mod's page, and everything's adjustable under Mods → TDO.

05 · MONEY & THE CITY

Money & The World

Your income comes from work, not loot. Gigs, fixer contracts, and bounties are where the real eddies are — selling off junk barely moves the needle. Later fixers pay much better than the early ones, and bringing cyberpsychos in alive pays more than killing them.

Vendors also react to your reputation in each district. The more jobs you do in a part of the city, the better the prices get with the vendors there, so it pays to put in work locally.

A few things are gated behind that work. Fixers won't start calling about a district until you've done some NCPD activity there. The list's added weapons and clothing are sold through Virtual Atelier, which you open from the Net menu on any computer. Fast travel and public transit cost time and money now, so getting across town isn't free.

There's new content woven in as well — a handful of extra quests, and NPCs that remember how you've treated them.

06 · BUILDS & CHROME

Builds, Perks & Cyberware

Merc Protocol adds a whole second layer of perks on top of the vanilla trees. You earn its points by doing merc work — gigs, cyberpsychos, side jobs, and NCPD assaults — and spend them in extra perk trees you open from the Character screen, under the Additional Perk Trees button. These perks give you new abilities rather than bigger numbers, and you can only earn about half of what you'd need to buy everything, so you'll have to make choices.

Cyberware carries more weight than vanilla. You're working with real slot limits and a humanity cost, so loading up on everything isn't an option — what you install is part of your build. On the Immersive profile, your cyberware also decides how much of your HUD you get. There's a lot of new chrome to find across the list, including a set of iconic cyberarms that each do something specific.

07 · DRIVING

Getting Around

Driving feels different than vanilla — handling has been reworked across cars and bikes, so they grip and steer differently, and it takes a minute to adjust. There's also a nitrous boost if you want a burst of speed; the key for it is in the table above.
